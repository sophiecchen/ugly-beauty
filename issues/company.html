Company Irresponsibility

With a new surge of young consumers interested in clean beauty and eco-friendly products, many companies recognize the advantages of green cosmetics. Rather than redesigning their products to be more eco-friendly, though, they use misleading marketing to attract customers.

Greenwashing

To put it simply, greenwashing is when a brand, product, action, etc. is deceptively marketed as environmentally friendly when it actually is not.

Companies, cross-industry, do it because this deceptive marketing gives increases their profits and reputation. 66% of people globally are willing to pay more for environmentally sustainable products while 72% of millennials, who make up a large portion of the beauty industry, are willing to pay more.

In the beauty industry, this manifests usually with green or nature-inspired packaging and labels like “natural” and “chemical-free.” Packaging with bright pictures of flowers, plants, or fruit, for example, help convince the consumer that the product is somehow connected with nature.

When looking out for greenwashing, watch for deceptive packaging and marketing. An image may illustrate the product with flowers or nature, but that does not necessarily mean that the brand is eco-friendly. In fact, these pictures are often ironically printed on colored plastic that cannot be recycled and harm marine life. For example, the cups below have "eco" printed on them in green. However, the cups are plastic, and thus are inherently damaging to the environment.

Deceptive words organic, natural, and chemical-free are signs of possible greenwashing. Neither organic or natural are FDA defined, and they may not apply to all ingredients. Having some “natural” or “organic” ingredients does not mean that there aren’t toxins in the product. “Nature makes plenty of toxic things,” notes Dr. Richard Blackburn, sustainability expert and chemist. Likewise, chemical-free does not mean toxin-free. Not all chemicals are harmful.

Brand Responsibility

Greenwashing is an especially potent issue in the overall problem of brand responsibility. Too often cosmetic brands blame the consumer for failing to recycle when the brand itself is often responsible for manufacturing waste and creating those difficult-to-recycle products in the first place.

It is important for consumers to make good decisions, but often this environmental action is interfering at the wrong place of the supply chain. The environment needs to be kept in mind from the very beginning of a product’s creation, so brands need to be checked and held accountable by the public.
