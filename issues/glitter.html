Glitter

Who doesn’t love a little sparkle and shine? Unfortunately, glitter, which is usually made of plastic, has a deadly impact on the environment. Scientists and environmental activists alike are calling for this treasured substance to be either regulated or banned completely.

Small But Deadly

Glitter, commonly found in cosmetics, is essentially small pieces of plastics. These microplastics are especially dangerous when washed down the drain. Because of their size, they can easily make their way into waterways and oceans, where they especially pose a threat.

Usually less than five millimeters, microplastics are especially appealing as food for oceanic animals. Whether coming from plastic in cosmetics or broken down plastic trash, estimates place the number of microplastics in the ocean up to 51 trillion pieces.

Microplastics can be consumed by fish, plankton, and similar marine life as well as seabirds, where plastic builds up in the stomach and cause starvation.

These microplastics could easily move up the food chain into our own dinner plates.

Glitter is usually made from aluminium and a plastic called PET. Dr Trisia Farrelly, an environmental anthropologist at Massey University in New Zealand, studied PET and found that the plastic, when broken down, could release disruptive chemicals and toxins in animals and humans that were linked to cancer and neurological diseases. She calls for an all encompassing ban on glitter.

“All glitter should be banned because it’s microplastic and all microplastics leak into the environment.”

Cosmetic glitter is especially dangerous. “These are literally 'down the drain' products. You put it on and you wash it off. They are made to be disposed of,” Dr. Farrelly explains. The rise of social media and glam makeup, though, has only made cosmetic glitter more and more popular, especially amongst young adults.

A ban on glitter may be difficult because of a current lack of concrete data on glitter. Regardless, cosmetic glitter is deadly to marine life As Dr. Farrelly says, avoiding cosmetic glitter is a “no brainer.”

Microbeads: An Unlikely Comparison

In 2015, a bill eliminating the use of microbeads in wash-off cosmetic products by mid-2017 was officially signed in the United States. In the United Kingdom, the restriction on microbeads went into effect in 2018. These measures came into effect after scientists discovered the dire consequences of microbeads.

Microbeads are also tiny plastic particles, usually smaller than two millimeters. In the beauty industry, they were widely used in products like face scrubs, shower gels, body wash, and toothpastes for exfoliation and scrubbing debris from the skin. Cheap and easy to produce, companies chose to include microbeads in their products rather than natural alternatives like apricot shells, jojoba beans, and pumice. The small spheres are meant to be washed down the drain after one use.

Like glitter, they can slip past wastewater treatment plants and travel into oceans, bays, and gulfs. Before the restrictions, about 808 trillion microbeads swirled down American drains daily. About 8 trillion of those microbeads escape wastewater treatment plants and were released into waterways.

Once in the ocean, microbeads added to the harmful effects of plastic pollution in the ocean. Their size allowed them to be easily be swallowed by even the smallest marine animals and travel up the food chain. They also transferred and released toxic chemical additives into the sea.

Some microbead pollutants have similar health concerns to glitter, as they have been linked to cancer, development issues, and birth defects.

The restrictions on microbeads are a step in the right direction. Unfortunately, plastic microbead bans are just a drop in the bucket. The bans will not make microbeads that are already in our waterways disappear, nor will they stop companies from including plastic or glitter in their cosmetics.

The similarities between microbeads and glitter are uncanny. While the former served as an exfoliant and the latter is used primarily for glitz and glam, both are microplastics with deadly effects. Stopping at microbead restrictions will not do much for our environment. If microbeads needed to be banned for their environmental impact, so does glitter.

A Biodegradable Alternative

Luckily, innovative companies have started creating and stocking biodegradable glitter. These glitters are usually compostable and plant-based, so they’ll break down rather than remaining in the environment for hundreds of years. Swap out your plastic, cosmetic glitter with these biodegradable glitter brands: EcoStarDust, Eco Glitter Fun, The Mermaid Cave, and Wild Glitter.

Other cosmetic chains like Lush have replaced all the glitter in their products with synthetic, biodegradable alternatives, or “starched-based lusters.” The company also advises to avoid being part of the problem by “checking labels of all your cosmetics to determine if they contain any plastic-based materials.” Lush, EcoStarDust, and similar brands show how sometimes, the most effective solution is top-down: action from brands to consumers.
