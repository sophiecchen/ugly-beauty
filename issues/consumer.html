Consumer Culture

A large part of environmental concerns come from the fact that we consume too much. Our world tells us that more is always better, but when it comes to more plastic and more toxins in the environment, this sentiment couldn't be more false.

A Need for More

We are constantly surrounded by media: advertising is on buses, videos, TV, and billboards. Along with flex culture, society today measures someone's prestige by their material goods. Companies use events like Black Friday to convice consumers to buy more and more.

The issue with having so many cosmetics is the cosmetics rarely get fully used. Each person only has one face, and having 20 lip products is unnecessary. Each cosmetic product leaves a footprint on the earth from not only the waste from packaging and containers after it has been bought, but also from the energy and water it takes to make the product.

Social Media

Within the last decade, social media has exploded. Its impact on our daily lives is potent, and along with this has come a new wave of consumer culture.

Companies are taking advantage of Instagram, YouTube, Twitter, etc. to promote their products. Whether using social media to connect with their customers or getting influencers to sponsor their products, this exposure has definitely helped businesses grow and people find a voice.

However, social media sponsoring is just another form of advertising, telling users that they need more, that having a certain item will fulfill them and make them happier. That item is usually unneeded, and buying it would just create more unnecessary waste.

Makeup and clothing hauls are another consumerist trend. Beauty and fashion influencers and creators buy dozens of products from a company at once, sometimes spending hundreds of dollars. After trying and reviewing each product for their viewers, too many influencers store these products away, rarely touching them again.

Hauling encourages a consumerist mindset that you can never have enough, that more is better.

Ultimately, social media and business can be a double-edged sword. It can be great exposure businesses like small, eco-friendly brands, but it can also pressure consumers into buying more unnecessary cosmetics, creating more waste that trashes the planet.
